SINGAPORE: If he had a mentor at a younger age, Mr Cheng Chai Hong believes he wouldn't have embarked on his undergraduate studies with the belief that†obtaining a degree was the only way to enter the working world with a competitive edge.†

The 31-year-old filmmaker "doesn't necessarily regret" his decision, but he thinks a professional mentor would have "near instantly" dispelled myths or clarified his fears.†

"(The benefits of obtaining a degree) may still be true for certain industries. But†this piece of wisdom handed down from my parents holds little water whenever I look around at myself and my peers, who are often either working in jobs that have little to do with their fields of study, or in fields that care little for your paper qualifications†and more about your work ethic," he told CNA.

"I felt that I didnít know how wide the world could be, and I had to make a decision on my direction in life with very little information available. So I want to provide the next generation with the opportunities I didnít have."

Mr Cheng has been a volunteer mentor with†non-profit organisation The Astronauts Collective (TAC) for about five years to help young people†who feel as lost as he did.†

The organisation works mainly with secondary and tertiary schools to encourage their students to think beyond grades and courses, and to start reflecting on their definitions of success and what makes a job meaningful.

"A lack of mentoring meant that the mistakes I made and experience gained were all my own. I didnít have the opportunity to learn through someone elseís experience, avoid mistakes that had already been made before, and forge ahead faster," he added.†

ìEssentially, I had to plough my own path in a lot of aspects of my life and career, which might not have been necessary with the help of a relevant mentor."

But while mentoring isnít a novel concept, and it would have benefited people like Mr Cheng,†it has never quite become mainstream practice.†

WHY THE LACK OF MENTORSHIP?†

In a Youth Mentoring Sentiment Poll conducted by the National Youth Council in February, only 11 per cent of 1,500 youth respondents aged 16 to 34 indicated that they have participated in formal mentoring programmes.†

However, more than 60 per cent of the respondents who hadnít participated in such a programme said they would be open to one.

Despite an appetite for mentoring programmes, there are several possible reasons why mentorship hasnít taken off here.†

First, a lack of awareness about how to begin a mentoring relationship impedes both potential mentors and mentees.†

ìYou want to help promote a culture of mentoring. A lot of mentoring is quite informal. For those who are more forward, they might ask if you can be a mentor to them. But for those who are not, and I think thatís the majority of youth, then they donít even know about these channels,î said Mr Alvin Tan, the Minister of State for the Ministry of Culture, Community and Youth and the Ministry of Trade and Industry.†

As the adviser to the Mentoring Alliance for Action that was launched in March, he aims to create a culture of mentoring in Singapore together with the alliance.†

One of the problems Mr Tan has observed†is the lack of awareness about what mentoring entails in the first place, which places like the alliance and†digital platform Youthopia try to solve.

ìMentees donít even know what mentoring is about. So you have to go really upstream to say, this is what mentoring is about. It is not about counselling. Rather, (these channels are)†where you can get mentoring resources and look for mentors,î he added.†

ìOn the mentor side, we assure you that you have a lot to offer in terms of your career and life choices, and (these are some places) where you can go to offer your time and energy, and we can pair you up with mentees.î
Minister of State Alvin Tan (right) presented a certificate of appreciation to a youth volunteer from the Mentoring Alliance SG at the National Mentoring Summit on Mar 26. (Photo: Ministry of Culture, Community and Youth)
Second, the ìextra workî, especially juggled alongside full-time jobs, can detract potential mentors from committing. There is also no guaranteed return on investment.

ìIf you took (up mentorship) in your own time, or you decided to do it because you want to help, thatís something else. But if someone assigned (mentoring) to you, you have to take time off. You already have daily targets and things you have to deliver. And then now you have to babysit this person,î said Dr Paul Lim, a lecturer of organisational behaviour and human resources at Singapore Management University (SMU).†

Dr Lim added that itís ìhard to measure the ROI (return on investment)î for mentorship.†

ìI think companies and organisations donít have a mechanism in place. They may take too much work, or they may not even know how to measure. So if you want mentorship to be mainstream, then you need to see metrics.î†

Third, there is the mentorís ìvery realî fear of being replaced.

ìThe mentor, sometimes, fears being replaced, especially if theyíre in the same organisation (as their mentee). They may think, if I teach you everything, one day you will use it against me. Itís a very real thing. Iíve heard a lot of Singaporeans talk about this,î said Dr Lim.†

Finally, the target market for mentoring programmes can be self-selecting, suggested Mr Wong Yi Fong, the co-founder of The Astronauts Collective.†

ìMany mentoring programmes tend to focus on youth who already want guidance and are proactively looking for mentors. However, youth who would benefit the most from having a mentoring relationship are those who donít understand the value of having mentors, hence theyíre not looking out for such opportunities for themselves,î said Mr Wong.†

ìThese might be students who are unmotivated or uninspired. They might find that they have no trusted adult figures in their lives they can turn to. These are the ones who would stand to gain the most out of a mentorship.î†

MENTORING MORE CRUCIAL IN THE PANDEMIC

Regardless of the hurdles and blindspots of mentoring in Singapore, there is no better time to ìrehash the topicî with work-from-home being the norm for the last one-and-a-half years, noted Dr Lim.†

ìPeople are just so inundated with a lot of things about COVID and working from home. I have students who graduate and have never been to the office, which gives a good context why (mentoring) is important,î he said.†

ìWe're going to get a whole cohort of graduates who really might not know how to deal with how things work in the organisation.î

At marketing agency MediaMonks, its managing director for Southeast Asia, Mr Joris Knetsch, agreed that the pandemic has made mentorship particularly challenging but also more important.

ìWith people working from home, not to mention the past year being particularly busy for a digital partner, itís been tough to create those connections or carve out the time to be a mentor amid increased levels of stress,î he said.†

ìBut that also reinforces the very need for these programmes and why theyíre so important. Making space for people to be there for each other and learn from one another precisely when new ways of thinking are so sorely needed.î
There is no better time to bring up the topic of mentorship again than in the pandemic, said one expert. (Photo: iStock)
To illustrate the significant role that career mentorship can play in the pandemic, Dr Lim pointed to the concept of ìorganisational socialisationî.†

According to the Oxford Handbook of Organisational Psychology, the concept is defined as ìa learning and adjustment process that enables an individual to assume an organisational role that fits both organisational and individual needsî.†

In the workplace, this translates into greater productivity.†

ìOrganisational socialisation is a long word that means getting up to speed and getting to understand how the company works. The faster the inexperienced hire gets up to speed, the more valuable they are. So your ROI (return on investment) is definitely faster,î said Dr Lim.†

ìYou can make a person do work and contribute rather than, you know, the person needs to be taught and guided. So definitely mentoring helps with that.î†

Research has also shown that mentoring leads to increased job satisfaction, he added.†

ìThis shows there is lower intention to leave the company. (Employees) tend to be more committed to the organisation.î†

While these benefits of mentorship arenít unique to life under COVID-19, they could combat the impending ìresignation tsunamiî due to the pandemic ñ†a term mentioned by senior lecturer from the National University of Singaporeís Business School, Ms Wu Pei Chuan, in a CNA commentary.†

In a separate commentary, she wrote that ìfour in 10 employees say they will leave their current roles in the next three to six monthsî, according to a McKinsey report published in September surveying five countries, including Singapore.†

Reasons for this include ìboreoutî, which Ms Wu described as a situation ìwhen employees feel disengaged and no longer see the point in what they are doing anymoreî.†

A culture of mentorship may address this, as it helps to keep employees engaged at work, according to experts and companies with formal and informal mentorship initiatives.†

EFFECTIVE MENTORSHIP BENEFITS BOTH PARTIES†

Dr Lim, whose research interests include mentorship, realised about five years ago that fresh graduates were beginning to ìdemandî mentoring in their career.†

As a result, he noticed companies pitching their employer value proposition as the fact that they provide mentoring to ìhookî candidates.†

This demand for mentorship even presented itself when he gave talks pre-pandemic. Occasionally, he would have a young person boldly come up from the audience to ask him to be their mentor.†

In the workplace, new hires can see that the company ìgenuinely caresî for their growth through the existence of formal and informal mentoring initiatives.

ìCare for employees is very important, especially for the younger millennials and older Gen Z. Mentorship signals that Ö (the company) really wants you to succeed,î said Dr Lim.†

Mentorship helps the company in terms of learning, and people ìmove fasterî when they learn fast, allowing the mentee to feel that theyíre learning much more, he added.
Mr Cheng Chai Hong, a volunteer at The Astronauts Collective, during one of his mentoring sessions for secondary school students before the pandemic. (Photo: The Astronauts Collective)
But experiencing the benefits of career mentoring can start much earlier in life, such as in secondary school when students begin to reflect on what their careers could look like, with the help of organisations like TAC.†

Three years ago, 17-year-old Arnav Yadur took part in one of TACís mentoring programmes on journalism.†

For six consecutive weekends, he and about 10 other schoolmates were mentored by a journalist to learn about her profession. It was his first taste of what a career in journalism could be, and it piqued his interest.††

Arnav kept in touch with his mentor, who wrote him a recommendation letter two years later that landed him a place in a mass communication diploma course through the Early Admissions Exercise.

ìI learnt how to interact with other people while interviewing, and how to finetune my word choices, as some may be sensitive to certain topics and have totally contrasting views,î he told CNA.†

ìI thought mentorship was just people giving you guidance into the aspects of what a certain career entails and it just stops there. But mentorship can be lifelong, as I am still in contact with my mentor till this date.î†

For Arnav, having a mentor in the field of oneís interest is ìnecessaryî as it helps ìmould your opinions and carve the right mindset to know exactly what youíd be putting yourself in moving forwardî. But to him, this only helps if you already ìhave a certain grasp of yourselfî.†

ìIf you are confused (about what you want to do), a mentor may be redundant. Mentorship must be at the right time and with the right person fit, in order to benefit you,î he said.†

On the other hand, Mr Cheng†doesn't need his mentees to have life figured out ñ†not even a little bit ñ†to benefit from mentorship. To him,†the very point of mentorship is to pass†a blueprint of mistakes, experience, knowledge and wisdom from one generation to the next.

ìEffective mentoring takes the burden of making the same mistakes over and over again off of the shoulders of the next generation, which allows us to make new mistakes, and society moves forward as a whole more efficiently,î he said.†

Mr Cheng has reaped the benefits of being a mentor, namely that it has made him a more effective and empathetic communicator.†

ìHaving to explain your experiences and technical knowledge to young people who arenít equipped to digest what youíre saying forces you to rethink and understand the basics of communication,î he said.

ìMost children at the schooling age havenít gone through the experiences mentors have, and thus might not easily see the importance of what we have to impart. The challenge for mentors is to figure out how to connect the wisdom weíre trying to impart with the studentsí own lived experiences.î

Being curious and having a healthy respect for what theyíre going through in their own lives is a good place to start, he added.†

As a mentor himself, TACís co-founder Mr Wong also appreciates having to reflect on his own career when he helps mentees work through their concerns and challenges.†

ìWhen I ask questions such as, ëWhy is this important to you? What do you find meaningful?í, I also have to attempt to answer these questions myself,î he said.
Reverse mentoring is a chance for younger or more junior employees bring their perspectives to the table and affect bigger decisions, said companies who have informal reverse mentoring initiatives. (Photo: iStock)
REVERSE MENTORING: INFORMAL BUT ëWORKS PERFECTLYí

While more ìtraditionalî models of mentorship are readily available if one knew where to look, the opposite mostly happens through informal initiatives.

Known as ìreverse mentoringî, the Harvard Business Review defined it as extending ìfar beyond just sharing knowledge about technologyî. It added that todayís reverse mentoring programmes focus on ìhow senior executives think about strategic issues, leadership, and the mindset with which they approach their workî.

In Singapore, the various companies CNA spoke to said reverse mentoring has helped their employees understand their voice is valued regardless of their age or years of experience.

ìOur approach to learning offers benefits on both sides of the equation. Older colleagues benefit by gaining perspective from those who are younger or newer to the industry, which is important in an ever evolving industry,î said Mr Knetsch from MediaMonks.†

ìReverse mentorship provides an opportunity for younger and newer colleagues to be recognised by bringing a fresh perspective to the table.î

Over at Wise, a financial technology firm, whether an employee is younger or older is ìreally irrelevantî, as they ensure their customers come first.†

ìThe benefit in having an open culture, where anyone can challenge and have a voice, no matter their age, means we can identify problems and opportunities quicker and work on getting the best solution,î said Ms Bianca Burke, the companyís head of people operations for Asia Pacific.†

ìWe believe our ego needs to come third after our customers first, and team second, so having a culture where people feel comfortable asking challenging questions is core to this principle.î

Ms Burke challenges the ìtraditional notionî that newbies or younger colleagues need to "do their time".†

ìThat notion is moot. We want everyone to have the opportunity to contribute without being held back by fear or hierarchy,î she said.

It's not just talk. Reverse mentoring results in "a win-win†situation" in practice too.

ìOne perfect example (of reverse mentoring in action) is when we have new sales hires. They are usually non-technical and sometimes have limited cybersecurity knowledge,î said†Mr Geoff Leeming, co-founder of cybersecurity firm Pragma.†

ìI have witnessed senior sales staff with more than 20 years of working experience sitting in with our security testing consultant, who happened to be more junior with only over five years of experience learning about the process and what goes behind the scenes during a project.†

ìIt's a win-win situation because the senior sales staff gets to learn some technical details to enable their sales process, while the junior staff gets to reinforce their knowledge and learn how to explain in non-technical terms, which is helpful when dealing with clients.î

Younger employees in Pragma benefit from reverse mentoring by learning leadership skills from someone more senior. Having the responsibility of being a mentor also boosts their self-confidence, while senior employees get to see things from new perspectives and learn to be more open-minded.

ìWe are in the cybersecurity industry, so there are always changes and new things coming up. Self-development and learning are important to keep up with the changes. So, reverse mentorship works perfectly,î added Mr Leeming.
The team at Kith & Kin Law Corporation, excluding their newest hire. Ms Angela Teo is seated to the extreme right. (Photo: Tan Shen Kiat)
At Kith & Kin Law Corporation, the team unwittingly started reverse mentoring the day its 35-year-old founder Tan Shen Kiat hired a stay-at-home mother 15 years older than him†as his first employee in 2019.†

The employee, Ms Angela Teo, then 48, had been†called to the bar before she became a full-time homemaker for 16 years, but never got to practise.†

When she returned to the legal field, she also obtained a graduate diploma in professional counselling so she could better walk alongside individuals facing difficult situations.†

The average law firm would look to law graduates, but Mr Tan wasnít keen. For Kith & Kinís area of specialty in estate planning and mental capacity law practice, he said Ms Teoís background ìmade senseî ñ†and her vast life experience was largely possible because of her age.††

ìWe only deal with estate planning and mental capacity related matters. That includes (working with) people with special needs and additional needs. Additional needs will be people who can function like a healthy able-bodied human being if special arrangements are made for them. Like people in wheelchairs, who have ramps, for example,î said Mr Tan.

ìThis area of work requires a lot more ability to connect with people, a bit more empathy. You need the technical knowledge, but I think technical knowledge can be picked up.î††

Mr Tan, who is also a practising lawyer, added that practice areas for law tend to be ìquite strait-lacedî, such as litigation, corporate, commercial or family law.†

ìOur practice area isnít something many people would know or treat as a specialised area. But I thought of her life experience, like Angela has kids, sheís a caregiver, future care recipient ... and she has a†niece with special needs,î he said.†

ìItís very difficult to find someone who has all these things and who also has a practising certificate, who got called to the bar. So I thought maybe we could give her a chance.î†

Ms Teo is the oldest employee on the five-person team today; aside from Mr Tan, the other team members in the team range from mid-20s to early 30s in age.

LESSONS BEYOND THE WORKPLACE†

In the end, whether itís traditional or reverse mentoring, those who have been mentor or mentee argued that a good career mentorship will end up imparting life lessons too.†

After more than five years, Mr Alvin Tan still holds the words of a former boss and mentor about humility close to his heart.††

The†MP for Tanjong Pagar GRC†was working at Goldman Sachs when his boss at that time ñ†Mr David Adelman, a former US ambassador to Singapore ñ†taught him a lesson for life.†

ìHe told me: 'When I leave this post, Iím just going to be David. When youíre an ambassador, minister, or whatever, people are going to (look at you) because of the office that you have the privilege to serve in. But donít conflate that with who you are',î Mr Tan told CNA.†

ì'Ultimately,†one day when you leave that office, you will just be Alvin, and Iíll just be David'," Mr Tan recounted. "'Donít be beholden to all of that.'î†

These words were ìvery releasingî for Mr Tan, who felt ìfreeî after hearing that.

ìItís a privilege to helm a particular office for a particular time, but I donít confuse it as ëthis is meí. (My former boss) showed me how to do it, and that itís possible,î he said.

In his current role as a politician, Mr Tan says he has benefited from the generosity of another person he considers a mentor: Senior Minister Tharman Shanmugaratnam.†

ìOccasionally, Iím in the Treasury Building. Iíll ask him if I can tap his brains. Itís quite informal, but then I'll just ask. He has decades of experience whereas Iím new to this. I donít think thereís a downside to asking. Heís always been very open, and very generous with his time,î said Mr Tan.†

ìI donít tell him that heís now my mentor, but more like, he gives that time and I ask sometimes for that time. He always takes the extra effort to say, 'How are you doing? Tell me more.' And I thought that was very generous of him.î†

Even though he has†benefited from the wisdom of more than one mentor throughout his career,†Mr Tan admitted he isnít sure whether the mentor figures in his life realise he considers them mentors.†Still, he has seen how mentorship has touched him and†wants others to experience the same.

To find a mentor, SMUís Dr Lim recommended that people look for ìa good role modelî instead.†

ìI did a study, and I realised almost all mentors would talk to their mentees about life issues. Ö Sure, you look for mentors for their competencies, what theyíre good at, whether theyíre famous (in their field), if theyíre a rainmaker for their company. But beyond that, are their practices ethical?î he said.†

ìHow they teach you to perform in the workplace has a huge impact on personal life ... on life decisions: Counselling (mentees) on family problems, should I marry this guy, how do I get my first home, how do I plan my finances, how do you survive (having kids). These are very real issues that go beyond the workplace.î

More than impacting individual lives, mentorship also provides the opportunity to reframe the ìlarger narrative of a self-sufficient, independent Singaporeanî, said Mr Cheng, who volunteers with TAC (The Astronauts Collective).†

This narrative stems from peopleís ìhang-ups over spoon-feedingî and letting the next generation become "soft", which naturally ìhardens the relationships between would-be mentors and menteesî.†

The mindset of ìI had to learn this the hard way, why do you get the easy way out?î also conditions people to be ìreluctant to share knowledgeî with each other and the next generation, he added.†

ìWe believe letting younger people make mistakes and ëlearn things the hard wayí is the only way to learn. I understand the appeal of such an approach, and can see how people often ignore the advice of elders in favour of making mistakes themselves so that they can absorb learnings more effectively,î he said.

ìBut there are entire realms of knowledge that can be handed down more efficiently than simply letting people ëgo and make mistakesí. Sometimes, itís simply about taking a more humane and empathetic approach.î

Based on what†Mr Cheng has learnt as a mentor over the last five years, however, getting Singaporeans to buy into mentorship might not be a walk in the park.

"Genuinely effective mentorship requires a healthy community and a welcoming and non-judgmental space.†And this sense of community is something thatís often lacking, especially in the Singaporean workplace and in professional environments.î
